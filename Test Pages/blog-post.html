SEO Trends in 2025: Search Intent and the Waning Role of Keywords

by Liam O'Rileyin SEO, Web Developmenton Posted on January 16, 2025January 16, 2025

My dear webmasters, 

Interestingly, in the last few months, I’ve begun hearing from Neil Patel of NP Digital and other sources that keywords hold less and less weight with the rise of AI in search engines. And there are other trends contributing to big changes coming in the near future.

Modern generative search engines are able to understand more context surrounding the topic of each webpage using synonyms and Natural Language Processing (NLP), and so don’t have to rely so heavily on specific keywords to determine if a post is relevant to a person’s search. 

That’s big. If you’ve been in marketing for any amount of time, you’ve been taught that keywords are the bread and butter of SEO, but in the immortal words of Bob Dylan, “the times they are a-changin’.” It looks like in the near future, keywords might just be the butter, and the bread will be our old friend, search intent.

Let’s Talk About SEO in 2025

“Keyword research has typically informed strategic SEO decisions. But it should no longer be seen as a standalone solution. SEOs can’t depend on keywords alone to rank on a SERP, never mind ranking effectively. Keywords need to be used in the context of user intent to be truly effective.” – Search Engine Land

Search Intent Is the “Why” Behind the Search

When a user types a query into Google or another search engine, there’s always a goal or a question behind the search. No one uses a search engine for no reason. We call this reason search intent, and it’s an increasingly important tool for SEOs.

As content creators, it can be easy to fall into the trap of making content for the sake of making it. But really, that just fills the web with noise and isn’t helpful to anyone. Every piece of content should fulfill a need or goal for the searcher, whether that be to alleviate a pain point, satisfy curiosity, entertain, or teach a skill, for example.

And now that search engines are getting better and better at recognizing context cues, it’s freeing up content creators to talk like actual people instead of keyword-happy marketing mongers. 

This Shift Could Help Us All Build a Better Web

When you understand why someone is submitting a search, you have a ready-made guide for making your content as helpful as possible to that user, and search engines’ algorithms tend to respond really well to that these days. All you have to do is answer the user’s question as directly as possible and provide any relevant information surrounding the topic that may also be helpful toward that user’s end goal. 

The game becomes predicting your audience’s search intent and responding to it with content that’s highly curated to fulfill that need or goal. And honestly, that’s what we all should have been doing all along. I’m personally looking forward to a web that isn’t full of hollow search results. How about you? 

So in 2025 and beyond, you’ll want to skill up in matching your users’ intent. I’ve already written about how to identify search intent here, and if you’re looking for more reading on the subject, check out this article by Semrush.

But wait, there’s more!

Long-Tail Keywords Are More Important Than Ever In SEO.

I’ve just finished telling you that keywords aren’t important anymore, haven’t I? Wrong. Keywords still have an honored place in the ranking process. It’s just that as our technology improves, other factors are holding more weight than they used to, and we need to shift the way we think about keywords as search trends change.

SEOs often give into the temptation to use short-tail keywords (key phrases 3-words long or fewer) because they’re often high-volume and easy to fit almost anywhere in your content. But that’s not always what’s best for your content or for users. This tendency can contribute to a black-hat SEO tactic called “keyword stuffing,” which refers to peppering your content with high-volume keywords whether they’re relevant or not.

For a long time, SEOs have known that long-tail keywords have less search volume, but can actually be easier to rank for due to their specificity. Longer-tail keywords narrow down the intended meaning and help search engines identify search intent to an uncanny degree of accuracy. And now, searchers (younger generations, especially) are using voice search, which tends toward long tail keywords as well.

Voice Search Is the Future of SEO.

Huddle Creative tells us that in 2024, “More than 20% of the searches in the Google App are done using voice search” and that there are more than 1 billion voice searches per month, and growing. Millennials and older generations were taught to type queries into search bars using a keyboard, but younger generations have grown up with smart devices in their homes. They’re used to shouting the name of a voice assistant and talking conversationally with search engines and AI. 

If you want your content to be seen in this world, you need to adapt your SEO strategy to accommodate for this trend. Specifically, you need to get familiar with “semantic search.” 

Search engines used to rely on lexical search, where they would literally match specific words (keywords) from content on your page to words in the user’s search query. AI is now capable of semantic search, using NLP to understand the greater context of sentences, paragraphs, and whole pieces of content and presenting them on the Search Engine Results Pages (SERPs). 

Semantic search has given rise to longer-tail keywords than ever because people just talk naturally and say exactly what they mean. The longer the query, and taking into account the order of words and the presence of sub-keywords, the search engine is able to interpret search intent with a high degree of accuracy.

That means that queries are getting more and more specific, and your content topics should be getting more specific to accommodate them. Your content needs to fulfill a very specific need in relation to the search, query, more specific than ever before.

This is all fantastic because as an SEO, you’re encouraged to create content that’s actually helpful to users. It’s a win-win. You can focus more on understanding your readers and answering their questions, less on trying to fit in buzz words as keywords like we’ve been taught, and your content will rank better for it!

User-Generated Content Is On the Rise This Year

Here’s one more tip as you begin your SEO journey for 2025: let users create content for you.

User-generated content is anything created by users that acts as content that helps your brand. This can be social posts, social shares, reviews, testimonials. According to the Global Banking & Finance Review, User-Generated Content can mean up to a 40% increase in conversion rate, so it’s nothing to snuff at.

There are endless options if you go this route. Just remember that people like to be involved in the things that they care about. So if you want people to care about your brand, give them opportunities to be involved with you! And another thing, search engines love seeing user engagement. Just saying.

Here Are Some Additional Tips for Writing Content In 2025.

Google another search engines have been cracking down on unhelpful content for a long time (Read about Google’s Helpful Content Update here.), and Google has shouted from the rooftops that it’s looking for EEAT: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust.

Many content creators find it difficult to understand how to inject these characteristics into their pieces, but it’s probably simpler than you think. Google is looking for cues that you are a trustworthy and authoritative source with experience and expertise. That’s all. 

So you just need to provide those cues by using personal anecdotes in your content, showing your expertise by including references to any awards or experiences you’ve had that may inform your claims, etc. You just need to show that you know your field and prove your claims using other trustworthy sources. Here are some more specific tips to show your own EEAT:

  • Use lots and lots of pronouns like “I,” “you,” “we,” etc. This states your experience and personal expertise with the topic.
  • Shoot for an approachable 9th grade education level in terms of readability. You want to sound like a person, not an encyclopedia. This keeps your content helpful to people of all backgrounds and helps keep your sentences clear and understandable.
  • Make sure that your claims don’t conflict with each other. This helps with both authoritativeness and trust.
  • Clearly disclose any personal profits, affiliations, or personal biases regarding your topic. This can help users make decisions and form a higher level of trust with your readers.

Remember that these tips can make a big difference in your overall rankings. Search engines have billions and billions of pieces of content to index and keep track of. Sometimes it helps for them to simply flag certain brands as trustworthy in their algorithms. That way, they save a little work with highly authoritative brands and don’t necessarily have to worry about their results being unhelpful or harmful to searchers.

Your goal is to become one of those brands that Google recognizes as consistently helpful and trustworthy. Once your domain has that stamp of approval, ranking individual pieces won’t be nearly as difficult. Of course, you’ll have to maintain your quality level in order to keep that reputation with search engines, but your diligence will be rewarded. 

Wrapping Up

More and more, people are making searches using their voice, so the semantics of search is changing to favor longer, more casual keywords. So instead of typing “best hotel near me” into Google, people are speaking search terms like “where’s a good hotel in Tacoma with a pool and rooms under two-hundred dollars a night.”

So when you’re searching for keywords, you can target more complete sentences, which are also easier to rank for because they’re more specific and target a smaller subset of searches than shorter, more general keywords.

If you take away anything from this article, let it be that as time goes on and technologies improve, keywords will still have their place in SEO, but search engines are prioritizing content that aligns as closely with users’ search intent as possible. Of course, there are ways to optimize your content, but more than ever, helpful content is being rewarded and content that exists only to serve analytics numbers only creates noise and in many cases, isn’t even index anymore.

So write about what’s important! Gone or the days of writing filler content, and arrived are the days of rating from your heart as an expert in your field. Hurrah!
